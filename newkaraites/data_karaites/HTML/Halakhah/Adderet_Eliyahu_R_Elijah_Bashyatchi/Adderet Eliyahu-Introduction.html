Introductory Note to this Edition of

R. Elijah ben Moshe Bashyatchi’s

Adderet Eliyahu

 

Eliyahu Bashyatzi’s Adderet Eliyahu (continued after his death by his student and brother-in-law Caleb Afendopolo, and originally circulated in handwritten manuscript form) has been published in three previous print editions. The first edition was printed in Constantinople by Gershom Soncino c. 1530-1531, four decades after the primary author’s death. A modern reprinting was done by Abraham Firkovich in 1835 in Gözleve (Eupatoria); another was done by Isaac Beim in 1870 in Odessa. All three printings were available to us as scanned documents; our warmest thanks are extended to Tomer Mangoubi for pointing us to the scan of the first edition.

 

The edition presented here uses the 1530-1531 first edition printing, דפוס ראשון, as its base text. Substantive divergences from that text found in the 1835 printing, דפוס א, or the 1870 printing, דפוס ב, are indicated in the footnotes. (Spelling-convention differences which do not affect meaning in any way, such as ענין vs. עניין or שוטה vs סוטה, are not noted.)

 

The distributions of some phrase and sentence markers (spaces, dashes, and colons) differ among the editions and are used inconsistently within each edition; these factors do not produce meaningful conflicts in the text’s content, and we have endeavored to handle them as sanely as possible throughout (so some markers have been altered in the base text to aid comprehension or flow, and to correctly group rhymed phrases in some poetic passages).

 

In addition to the printed texts themselves as they were typeset, our edition also reflects our access to marginalia: the scans of the first edition and the 1835 edition which were available to us were produced using copies in which unknown individuals made marginal notes, corrections, and insertions. In most cases this content will simply be footnoted; however, any marginal additions in 1530 that are corroborated by the modern printings have also been retained in the base text in square brackets. (1530 marginal corrections of contextually-obvious errors are also reflected in the base text; e.g., in one place all the printed texts have שביעיות, “sevens,” but the context, in the middle of the “sevens” passage, is a tangent explicitly discussing “eights” like Shemini Atzeret and the Yovel year - there, 1530’s marginal emendation to השמיניות, “eights,” is accepted in brackets for clarity, and the change is footnoted.)

 

Each of the three printings has its own set of errata, haplographies, and dittographies (and the modern printings were censored in a handful of places). Most often, one (or both) of the modern editions is missing content that appears in the 1530 printing. In rare instances, our 1530 base text דפוס ראשון is the one with an obvious error or omission (missed by the marginal corrector) that affects the coherence of the text, and which can be resolved by consulting the modern printings; where necessary, this content is inserted in the base text in square brackets and footnoted. (If the meaning of the text is not greatly affected, the content is simply footnoted.)

 

All the printed editions make frequent use of space-saving abbreviations and clipped (shortened, with a ') words. For the benefit of modern readers of the text, and for the benefit of readers searching within this text as a digital edition, most abbreviations have been unpacked (e.g., וי"א - ויש אומרים), and clipped-off suffixes have been restored (e.g., כעבדי' - כעבדים). Some names of people (e.g., “Comtino”; “Al-Battani”) or places (e.g., the name for Constantinople “Kosdina”) that were spelled with slight variations in different passages have been standardized to the most common spelling. Some of the Biblical quotes have been corrected (always with a footnote indicating what was printed in the original text).

 

Biblical verse references are inserted in round parentheses in the main text (though a verse cited repeatedly within a passage is often only parenthetically referenced the first time or at intervals throughout, or when additional words from the same verse are quoted). These parenthetical references have been omitted in some poetic sections that make use of Biblical phrases and verse fragments (often with modifications to fit the poem) as “building blocks” of the composition, disjointed from their original Biblical context.

 

Where feasible, charts and tables are reproduced from the first edition. Certain charts and tables are printed more legibly in the modern editions, and we have used those for the sake of readability - so some will look different from others. Minor cleanup work was done on some of these images.

 

In cases where it might be to the reader’s benefit to see one or more of the printings “inside” with their own eyes, we have clipped the relevant materials out of the scanned copies and placed these excerpted images in the Appendixes (נספח).

 

Firkovich’s 1835 edition appends the text of two letters mentioned in the text of the Adderet (to which the reader is referred for further detail on their respective topics). These two letters, Iggeret Ha-Tzom and Iggeret Gid Ha-Nasheh, are also included here in the Appendixes, along with a third letter, printed by Firkovich alongside the other two but left untitled, which we have titled Iggeret Ha-Yerushah.

 

As with any digital text, especially one of this size, it is reasonable to assume that some novel errors may have crept in during the editing process. Readers who suspect they have noticed typographical errors or incorrect citation references are invited to inform the editor at bernsteinmsn@gmail.com so that any potential errors can be fixed within the digital edition.
