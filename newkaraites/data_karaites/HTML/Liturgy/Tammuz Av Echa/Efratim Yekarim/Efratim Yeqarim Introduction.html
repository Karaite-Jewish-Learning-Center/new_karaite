Category: Liturgy

Genre: Liturgical poetry

Sub-genre: Qinot (lamentation poems)

Occasion: Tenth of Av

Source: Vilna Karaite Siddur, vol. 1, p. 133

Acrostic: אהרן חזק ואמץ,

“Aharon, be strong and mighty!”

Meter: Five full syllables per stanza; half-vowels (sheva and ḥataf) do not count.

Rhyme: In each stanza, the first three lines rhyme with each other, and the fourth has

the rhyme –rim throughout the poem.

Notes: In the second stanza (beginning המית), the poet refers to the Jewish people as “sons of sorcerers,” a term that refers to the Israelites in a very negative way in its Biblical context (Isaiah 57:3), but seems to be neutral here. In the same stanza, the poet refers to מעון ראש כל ההרים, “the heavenly lair, high above all mountains.” This is probably an allusion to the concept of a heavenly Temple, because the expression ראש כל ההרים “high above [literally: the head of] all the mountains” seems to allude to Isaiah 2:2, about the (earthly, Jerusalem) Temple: The Lord’s House will be at the head of all the mountains.

In the third stanza (beginning רחל), the word קֶרַח, “ice,” might be a copyist’s error for קָרָה, “cold [wind],” since the line alludes to Job 37:9, which uses this word, not קֶרַח.

In the ninth stanza (beginning אמלל), there is a problem with the meter in the third line; accordingly, the word הָהָר is probably a copyist’s error for simply הַר. Also in the ninth stanza, in the words אשר בזאו נהרים, “which the ‘rivers’ plundered,” taken from Isaiah 18:2, the poet seems to be using the word “rivers” as an epithet for the gentile nations; this is also how the Rabbanite commentator Isaac Abravanel understands the verse in Isaiah in his book Mashmia‘ Yeshu‘a, chapter 3.

In the tenth stanza, “when the bedspread was too small” is a reference to Isaiah 28:20,

where, according to Aaron ben Joseph’s commentary Mivḥar Yesharim (and some other commentators), it refers to adultery, meaning that the bed is too small to fit a husband, a wife, and an adulterer, and is a metaphor for when the Israelites brought an idol into the Temple. When this happened, then the Israelites were punished with “war at the gates,” and the destruction of the Temple.
